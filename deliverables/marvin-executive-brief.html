Overview

Executive summary

Principal's proposed deal structure

Marvin Smith, as principal, has publicly solicited a commercial real estate development partnership for a boutique hotel with a ground-floor Ollie's Trolley restaurant at 1601–1607 Central Avenue in the West End, Cincinnati. The proposed deal provides 49% ownership of the completed development to a qualifying developer in exchange for technical expertise and pre-development funding, with the principal retaining 51%.

Site facts

The principal controls three contiguous parcels totaling 0.138 acres (~6,010 sf) on the northeast corner of Central Avenue and West Liberty Street, consolidated under a single ownership. The parcels are zoned MG-T (Manufacturing General — Transect overlay), sit within Cincinnati's CRA program area, and are not in an Opportunity Zone, TIF district, or historic district. Adjacent parcels on the same block are held by the City of Cincinnati, the Hamilton County Land Bank, and several private owners — materially relevant to any program exceeding the principal's current footprint.

Purpose of this memorandum

This document walks through the proposed development opportunity in sequence: the publicly stated proposition, site fundamentals, the program-to-site capacity relationship, the adjacent parcel landscape, the capital architecture required to deliver the project, pre-development capital demands, available principal equity structures, and a proportionality view of the proposed ownership split. At the conclusion, the memorandum synthesizes these facts into a strategic summary, identifies risks for the principal's consideration, and outlines options for the path forward. Figures presented throughout are directional ranges appropriate to a Phase Zero pro forma lite; sources and methodology are listed at the end of the document.

The Proposition

Project as stated

The development proposition, as publicly circulated, is a boutique hotel and restaurant on Central Avenue in the West End, with the principal retaining 51% ownership in exchange for contributing land and vision, and the developer providing technical execution and the entirety of pre-development capital for the remaining 49%. The public rendering depicts an eight-story masonry mixed-use building with an urban streetwall presentation — a 60–80 key boutique program.

"Wanted: A Commercial Real Estate Developer to build a boutique Hotel and Ollie's Trolley Restaurant at the current location. West End, 1601–07 Central Avenue. Provide all technical expertise from start to finish. Must have your own funding mechanism to finance predevelopment for 49% ownership of development." — Source: principal's public solicitation

This memorandum evaluates that proposition against the site's actual legal, physical, and capital realities, and recommends adjustments to the structure, scale, and go-to-market approach needed to convert the vision into a closeable transaction.

Structural detail
I.

Land sale + anchor tenant

Principal sells the three parcels at appraised value at closing and retains Ollie's Trolley as the ground-floor anchor under a long-term below-market lease (50% of market, 20 years + renewal options), with naming rights and exterior co-branding. Cash exit from the land position with zero construction or operating risk; no participation in hotel appreciation.

II.

Land as equity contribution

Parcels contributed to the development entity at appraised value (~$700K), received as pari-passu equity alongside sponsor GP and LP capital. Restaurant lease and naming rights retained from Option I. Base-case payout (TDC $29M vs. stabilized value $25M) pencils close to Option I's cash — and because it pays 5–7 years later, present-value may favor Option I. The structure's real value emerges in upside scenarios where the project appreciates past TDC (see Waterfall tab).

III.

Preferred equity + residual

Same land contribution as Option II, structured as preferred equity: ~$56K/yr preferred return paid before common equity, plus a 3–5% residual participation after the preferred is satisfied. Restaurant lease and naming rights retained. Delivers in-period cash yield and a safer waterfall position; back-end smaller than Option II if the project materially outperforms.

10
Proportionality Analysis

The 49% question

In standard hotel joint venture economics, ownership percentages typically track capital contributed, guarantees signed, and risk absorbed. The purpose of this section is to quantify, for reference, what a 49% equity position implies under proportional contribution — so the principal can evaluate the proposed structure alongside the facts. This is analysis, not judgment: some developers structure deals outside standard proportionality for strategic, relationship, or positioning reasons.

Proportionality reference

If 49% were measured against proportional capital and risk, the contribution implied would be:

  • Principal's land at appraised value (~$700K), plus
  • If measured against total project cost, roughly $13.5–14.7M of contributed value into a $28–30M deal, plus
  • If measured only against the equity layer, several million dollars of cash equity plus meaningful guarantee support, plus
  • 49% share of pre-development risk — approximately $300–540K of at-risk capital deployed over 15–18 months before closing.

Risk for principal consideration: Where an equity position is materially outside proportional contribution, the pool of interested developers may be smaller. Developers who do engage may require offsetting concessions (higher fees, preferred positions, or control provisions). This is a market risk, not a rule — but it is the risk profile the principal should weigh against the retained ownership level.

Synthesis

Strategic assessment

The preceding sections present the site, program, capital architecture, and equity mechanics of the proposed development. The summary below organizes those facts into observations and risks for the principal's consideration. Recommendations are framed as options, not prescriptions — the principal directs strategy.

Key observations

A
Program and site are not matched at current land position. The principal controls 0.138 acres (~6,010 sf). The rendered 60–80 key boutique hotel typically requires approximately 0.63–0.70 acres (~25,000–30,000 sf) to accommodate the building, ground-floor restaurant, parking, and loading (source: hospitality development industry benchmarks). The depicted program cannot be delivered on the current parcels alone.
B
Adjacent land is disproportionately publicly controlled. The City of Cincinnati and Hamilton County Land Bank hold 0.302 adjacent acres accessible through negotiated disposition processes. Distressed private holders (M C Medina Properties, SW Ohio Group) hold an additional 0.258 acres. The full-block assemblage reaches the required program scale via a combination of public-private conveyance and distressed private acquisition (source: Hamilton County Auditor, CAGIS).
C
The subsidy profile is promising but unconfirmed. A negotiated commercial CRA package appears available (source: ChooseCincy commercial CRA program overview). NMTC eligibility, City of Cincinnati gap financing, public-land implied equity, and a potential HTC layer are plausible but not committed. Each requires active pursuit through Phase Zero diligence and application.
D
Project economics at target scale are directionally viable but carry a modeled ~$8M indicative-sources gap. Total development cost at the ~70-key program is modeled at $29M (source: RSMeans Cincinnati 2026, industry ratios for hospitality Type III). Indicative-only sources (senior debt, sponsor GP equity, LP equity, principal land contribution) total approximately $21M. The remaining $8M gap is expected to be closed by the modeled subsidy layer, which is itself unconfirmed at this stage.
E
Risk: equity structure may narrow the developer pool. The 49% / 51% split as publicly stated places the developer's equity position outside standard proportionality to capital and risk contributed (see §10). This does not mean the structure is unworkable — some developers may accept it for strategic, positioning, or relationship reasons. The risk for the principal to weigh is that a smaller pool of developers may engage, or that those who do may request offsetting concessions (development fee, promoted interest, or control provisions).
F
Alternative structures are available if principal prefers broader developer interest. The three structures in §09 present options that align ownership with proportional contribution while preserving the principal's economic and strategic upside — land as equity, anchor tenant lease, naming rights, preferred return. Principal may elect any of these, the originally proposed 51/49 structure, or a hybrid. The structural choice is the principal's.

Path forward — options for consideration

The facts above suggest the next highest-value action for the principal is converting the opportunity from a public solicitation into a structured development package prior to engaging developers. A Phase Zero program — feasibility and capacity analysis, zoning pathway confirmation, hotel market study, adjacent-parcel assemblage strategy, subsidy-stack diligence, and a developer RFP package — would produce the underwritten foundation against which any equity structure (51/49 or otherwise) becomes negotiable on defensible terms. Scope and engagement are outlined in §12. The principal may also proceed under the current solicitation without structured diligence — this is a risk / speed tradeoff the principal is positioned to judge.

Advisory Scope

Recommended engagement

The Co-Llab Group is not a developer and does not propose to be the project's equity partner. The role proposed here is as Phase Zero advisor to the principal — producing the structured, underwritten package required to attract a qualified developer on defensible terms.

Scope of work

Feasibility and capacity analysis, zoning pathway confirmation, market study, subsidy eligibility determination (commercial CRA, NMTC, TIF), adjacent parcel assemblage strategy, City & Land Bank pre-engagement, developer RFP package, and structured equity term sheets suitable for negotiation with qualified developers.

Deliverable posture

A diligence-cleared development opportunity with a defined capital architecture, a controlled land position, and principal economics that reflect proportional contribution. The principal meets developers from a position of documented control, not an open solicitation.

Engagement structure: fixed-fee for Phase Zero deliverables, with an optional success fee payable at construction close. Incentives are aligned with deal closure on terms the principal approves — no position is taken on any particular equity structure prior to the principal's direction.

13
For Principal Consideration

Strategic decisions

No decision is requested in this memorandum. Three questions govern the structure and sequencing of the engagement; the principal's positions on each drive the recommended path and the scope of the Phase Zero work.

What is the principal's primary objective?

Cash at closing, long-term equity appreciation, operational control, Ollie's Trolley continuity in the development, brand presence, or developer-of-record status? Each objective optimizes to a different structure. The recommended package is calibrated to the principal's stated priority.

Is the principal prepared to pursue adjacent parcel assemblage?

The rendered program requires assemblage with City, Land Bank, and distressed private holders. This adds 12–18 months and requires sustained public-sector engagement. Path A remains available on the existing 6,010 sf but with substantially weaker economics and limited operator interest.

Will the principal engage Phase Zero advisory?

The Phase Zero package is the deliverable set that converts this from a public solicitation into a closeable transaction. Scope and fee are calibrated to the path selected in the two questions above.

The corner at 1601 Central is a valuable position. Ollie's Trolley carries brand equity in the neighborhood. The surrounding public land creates an assemblage pathway few West End sites can access. The principal is positioned to direct the strategy, the structure, and the pace of the path forward — this memorandum is intended to inform those choices.
